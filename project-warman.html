Warman Design & Build Challenge

Led a team of three representing UTS at the international Warman finals — one of 15 universities competing across Oceania. The 2024 challenge: autonomously collect and deposit six tennis balls from preset positions into a central hole as quickly as possible.

Overview

The Warman Design & Build Challenge is a yearly international competition run by Engineers Australia, targeted at second-year mechatronics and mechanical engineering students. In 2024, fifteen universities across the Oceania region competed. The task required building a robot that could navigate a fixed course, collect six tennis balls from preset positions, and deposit them into a central hole — as fast as possible.

As team lead, I was responsible for the overall design strategy as well as personally designing and building the chassis, drivetrain, a custom 3D-printed worm-drive gearbox, the carbon fibre 900mm unfolding arm, and the electronics board.

Design strategy

The core design philosophy was to minimise degrees of freedom. Every additional actuator adds complexity, potential failure modes, and control overhead. The resulting robot used just six actuators in total: two encoded DC gearmotors for the drivetrain, three servos for ball collection and deposit mechanisms, and one stepper motor to drive the arm gearbox.

This constraint-first thinking shaped every subsystem decision — from the choice of a fixed-geometry arm (rather than a multi-segment articulated one) to the waterjet-cut tube-and-gusset chassis construction that prioritised stiffness and a low centre of gravity over flexibility.

Drivetrain & control

Accurate, repeatable straight-line motion is critical in a timed autonomous task where the robot must collect balls from fixed positions. The drivetrain used closed-loop cascaded PID control with velocity and differential steering feedforward terms.

Rather than relying on a theoretical motor model, I generated over 500 empirical data points mapping PWM input to measured velocity under various load conditions. This dataset was used to tune the feedforward parameters, giving the robot consistent and predictable motion from the first run.

The cascaded structure — an outer position loop driving an inner velocity loop — allowed precise point-to-point moves with controlled deceleration and near-zero steady-state error, minimising time lost to overshoot or correction at each ball pickup position.

Arm & gearbox

The arm needed to extend from a compact stowed configuration to a 1-metre reach in under two seconds, lock rigidly for ball collection, then retract — all reliably, repeatably, and without software complexity.

A custom worm-drive gearbox was 3D-printed and driven by a NEMA 23 stepper motor, producing a theoretical output torque of 24 Nm — sufficient to propel the arm 160 degrees in just over a second. The worm gear's self-locking property meant the arm held its extended position without holding current, reducing thermal load on the driver.

The arm itself was fabricated from carbon fibre in a multi-segment hinged configuration with mechanical locking pivots. Hinges snap and lock at full extension, providing a rigid structure without additional actuation. The whole deploy sequence — unfold, lock, collect — runs open-loop from a single stepper command.

Key skills developed
